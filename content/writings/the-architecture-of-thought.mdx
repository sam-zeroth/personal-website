I've been thinking a lot about how we think. Not in the philosophical sense — in the structural sense. How does the architecture of cognition compare to the architectures we build in software?

The parallels are closer than you'd expect.

## Nodes and edges

Every thought you have exists in relation to other thoughts. When you remember a song, you don't retrieve it from some isolated memory bank — you follow a chain of associations. The smell of rain, a car ride, a specific summer. The song is a node in a network, not a file in a folder.

This is fundamentally different from how we've traditionally organized information in software. Databases are tables. File systems are trees. But the mind is a **graph**.

## Why this matters for what we build

When I started building the 3D brain visualization for this site, I wanted it to be more than a gimmick. The brain metaphor reflects something real about how I think about my work — everything is connected, and the connections are as interesting as the nodes.

> The medium is the message. If you want to communicate that ideas are interconnected, don't put them in a list.

Each region of the brain maps to a different kind of thinking:

- **Frontal lobe** → Work. Planning, execution, decision-making.
- **Parietal lobe** → Personal. Spatial awareness, self-perception.
- **Temporal lobe** → Writings. Language, memory, expression.
- **Occipital lobe** → Contact. Vision, seeing the world, connecting outward.

These aren't arbitrary assignments. They reflect how I actually experience these different modes of being.

## The technical connection

Neural networks in machine learning are, of course, inspired by biological neural networks. But we've abstracted away most of the interesting properties. Real neurons are:

- **Sparse** — most connections are inactive at any given moment
- **Plastic** — connections strengthen or weaken with use
- **Noisy** — randomness is a feature, not a bug

Our artificial networks are dense, fixed, and deterministic. We've kept the math and lost the philosophy.

## What I'm trying to build

This site is an experiment in bringing some of that organic quality back to how we present information. Not a portfolio — a **mind map**. Not a blog — a **thought stream**. The 3D visualization isn't decoration. It's an argument about how information wants to be organized.

I don't know if it works yet. But that's the fun part.
