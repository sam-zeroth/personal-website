Every pixel is a word. Every interaction is a sentence. Every experience tells a story.

This isn't a metaphor — it's a framework for thinking about design that has fundamentally changed how I approach my work.

## The language analogy

When we learn to write, we learn grammar before style. Subject, verb, object. Paragraph structure. Only after mastering the basics do we develop voice. Design works the same way.

The grammar of design is:

- **Hierarchy** — what should the eye see first?
- **Consistency** — does the same element always mean the same thing?
- **Spacing** — are related things close and unrelated things far?
- **Contrast** — is the important thing visually distinct from the unimportant thing?

These are the rules you learn first. Most designers stop here. The interesting work begins when you start breaking them intentionally.

## What design communicates

Every design decision sends a message, whether you intend it or not:

**A minimalist interface says**: "We respect your attention. We've done the hard work of deciding what matters."

**A dense dashboard says**: "You're a power user. We trust you to handle complexity."

**A playful animation says**: "We don't take ourselves too seriously. This should be fun."

**A brutalist layout says**: "Content over chrome. Substance over style."

The problem isn't any one of these messages — it's when the message doesn't match the intent. A banking app with playful animations sends mixed signals. A children's game with a minimalist interface feels cold.

## Designing this site

When I designed this portfolio, I wanted to communicate something specific: **thinking is dimensional**. Ideas don't live in flat lists — they exist in space, with connections and depth.

That's why the brain. Not because it's flashy (though it is), but because it's the right metaphor. When you click on a region, you're not navigating a menu — you're exploring a topology of thought.

The drawer panel is deliberately understated. Once you've made the journey into a brain region, the content should speak for itself. No competing animations. No distracting UI. Just words, organized clearly, breathing.

> Good design is invisible. The user should be thinking about the content, not the container.

## The hardest part

The hardest part of design-as-communication isn't making things pretty. It's being honest about what you're trying to say.

If you don't know your message, your design will be incoherent — a collection of aesthetic choices that don't add up to anything. Pinterest-board design. It looks good in screenshots but feels hollow in use.

Start with the message. Let the pixels follow.
